## Executive Summary

I document a convergence of regulatory developments that creates a strategic inflection point for Brazil's emerging virtual asset framework. On June 6, 2026, six U.S. senators sent a joint letter to the Federal Reserve, OCC, and FDIC arguing that the Basel Committee's 1,250% risk weight on Bitcoin functions as a de facto ban on bank crypto participation. One day later, on June 7, 2026, more than 100 companies — including Coinbase, Kraken, Circle, Ripple, Andreessen Horowitz, Elliptic, and Uniswap — signed a joint letter urging the U.S. Senate to bring the Digital Asset Market Clarity Act to a floor vote without delay.

I flag this convergence not as a problem for Brazil, but as a strategic window. Brazil has constructed one of Latin America's most sophisticated virtual asset regulatory frameworks through Lei 14.478/2022 and BACEN Resolutions 519, 520, and 521. As the BACEN advances Consulta Pública 126 — which proposes prudential capital rules for firms with digital asset exposure aligned with international Basel standards — the global Basel framework itself is entering active revision. This creates an opportunity for Brazil to participate in shaping the next generation of prudential standards rather than importing a version that advanced economies are already moving to reform.

## I. The Basel 1,250% Framework: Construction and Context

The Basel Committee on Banking Supervision finalized its crypto-asset capital standard in late 2022 through BCBS 405. Under this framework, digital assets are classified into two groups. Group 1 covers tokenized traditional assets and stablecoins meeting strict reserve and redemption criteria. Group 2 covers all other crypto assets, including spot Bitcoin — and it is here that the 1,250% risk weight applies.

The mathematical implication is precise and consequential. A 1,250% risk weight, multiplied by the Basel minimum capital ratio of 8%, produces a capital requirement equal to 100% of exposure. In practical terms: every dollar of spot Bitcoin held by a bank requires one dollar of Tier 1 capital held against it. An additional exposure limit applies — banks' Group 2 holdings should generally not exceed 1% of Tier 1 capital, with full Group 2b treatment applied when exposure reaches 2%.

I note that the 1,250% figure was not arbitrary. It was calibrated during a period of significant crypto market stress, high institutional uncertainty, and limited regulatory infrastructure for digital asset custody and monitoring. The Basel Committee applied the highest possible risk weight — the same used for opaque securitization tranches — as a conservative baseline while global standards for digital asset supervision were still being developed.

The framework was rational at the time of its construction. What I document here is that the conditions under which it was built have changed materially.

## II. The U.S. Senate Pressure: Six Senators, One Hundred Companies

On June 6, 2026, Senators Bill Hagerty, Dan Sullivan, Cynthia Lummis, Bernie Moreno, Jon Husted, and Ted Budd sent a joint letter to the Federal Reserve, OCC, and FDIC. The letter argued that the Basel 1,250% risk weight functions as a de facto ban on meaningful bank participation in digital assets, and called for a new U.S. capital framework that treats digital asset holdings on the basis of their actual risk characteristics rather than their asset classification.

The senators pointed specifically to joint guidance issued by U.S. banking regulators in March 2026 regarding tokenized securities — which clarified that capital treatment should be based on the underlying asset's risk profile rather than the technology used to record ownership. They argued this technology-neutral principle should apply more broadly to digital assets, including spot Bitcoin.

On June 7, 2026, Stand With Crypto, the Blockchain Association, the Crypto Council for Innovation, and The Digital Chamber jointly submitted a letter to Senate Majority Leader John Thune and Minority Leader Charles Schumer, signed by more than 100 companies including Coinbase, Kraken, Circle, Ripple, Andreessen Horowitz, Elliptic, ZKsync, Uniswap, Paradigm, and Wintermute. The letter urged the Senate to bring the Digital Asset Market Clarity Act to a floor vote without delay, warning that absent a clear U.S. framework, digital asset activity would migrate to "offshore jurisdictions with less transparency, weaker consumer protections, and limited accountability."

I flag the joint industry letter not as a lobbying document but as a signal of the scale of institutional alignment behind CLARITY Act passage. When custodians, exchanges, market makers, blockchain analytics firms, and venture capital investors submit a unified position to bipartisan Senate leadership, it indicates that the legislative pathway — however contested — has reached a level of maturity that warrants serious regulatory attention from aligned jurisdictions.

## III. The Structural Tension: CLARITY Act and Basel Capital Rules

I identify a structural tension that has not yet been widely addressed in the regulatory literature.

The Digital Asset Market Clarity Act, which passed the U.S. House by a vote of 294 to 134 on July 17, 2025, would authorize banks to engage in a broader range of digital asset activities, establish clear jurisdictional boundaries between the SEC and CFTC for digital asset supervision, and create registration pathways for digital asset brokers, dealers, and exchanges under federal law.

The CLARITY Act, in other words, is built on the policy assumption that banks will participate meaningfully in digital asset markets. The Basel 1,250% framework, as currently written, makes that participation economically impractical for any institution operating under standard Tier 1 capital constraints.

These two frameworks are not technically incompatible — a bank could, in principle, comply with both simultaneously. But the economic reality is that compliance with Basel Group 2b capital treatment renders the activities contemplated by the CLARITY Act prohibitively expensive at any meaningful scale. The senators' letter makes precisely this argument: that the Basel standard, as applied to spot Bitcoin, was calibrated for an asset class that lacked the institutional infrastructure, market liquidity, and regulatory oversight that now exists.

I apply the FATF Risk-Based Approach (Recommendation 1) as an analytical lens here. The RBA requires that regulatory requirements be proportionate to the actual risks presented by the activity or asset in question. A risk weight calibrated for opaque, illiquid, unrated securitization tranches may not accurately reflect the risk profile of a publicly traded, highly liquid asset operating on transparent, auditable blockchain infrastructure — particularly when that asset is now subject to robust AML/CFT obligations, sanctions screening requirements, and Travel Rule compliance frameworks in multiple major jurisdictions.

## IV. Brazil's Framework: Sophisticated Construction, Global Alignment

Brazil has built one of the most comprehensive virtual asset regulatory frameworks in Latin America. I document the key structural elements:

**Lei 14.478/2022** established the foundational legal basis for virtual asset regulation and assigned primary regulatory authority to the Banco Central do Brasil.

**BACEN Resolution 519** imposed enhanced AML/CFT controls for PSAVs, including identification of self-hosted wallet owners, verification of origin and destination of crypto assets, and due diligence on foreign PSAVs. Monthly reporting obligations to BACEN took effect on May 4, 2026.

**BACEN Resolution 520** governs the formation, operation, and supervision of PSAVs/SPSAVs, establishing service modality definitions, client asset segregation requirements, cybersecurity standards, proof-of-reserves mechanisms, and AML/CFT policies proportionate to risk.

**BACEN Resolution 521** integrates virtual asset transactions into Brazil's foreign exchange regime, requiring authorization for cross-border activities and setting minimum capital requirements. PSAVs must hold between R$10.8 million and R$37.2 million in capital, calibrated to activity type and scale.

**Normative Instruction 739** adds an independent audit requirement — conducted by entities registered with the CVM — as a prerequisite for PSAV licensing. Without the audit, no license.

**Consulta Pública 126** proposes prudential capital rules for firms with digital asset exposure, explicitly aligned with international Basel standards.

I flag Consulta Pública 126 as the specific point of intersection between Brazil's framework and the global Basel reform debate. It is an open consultation — which means it represents an active opportunity for engagement, not a concluded regulatory determination.

## V. The Strategic Window: Participating in Standard-Setting

I do not document the Basel 1,250% reform pressure as a risk to Brazil's framework. I document it as a strategic opportunity.

Brazil's regulatory timeline creates a specific alignment. Consulta Pública 126 is open precisely as the global debate over Basel crypto capital standards enters its most active phase. The Federal Reserve is expected to publish its Basel III crypto implementation proposal, followed by a 90-day public comment period. The Basel Committee itself has acknowledged the need for targeted review of its crypto-asset framework.

This means that the prudential capital rules Brazil is developing for PSAVs under Consulta Pública 126 will be finalized at approximately the same time that the international baseline is being renegotiated. Brazil is not a passive recipient of these standards — it is a sophisticated financial market regulator with deep Basel Committee engagement, and it operates the largest crypto market in Latin America, ranking fifth globally in the 2025 Chainalysis Crypto Adoption Index with $318.8 billion in crypto value received in the preceding twelve months.

I apply the following analytical framework to identify what a constructive Brazilian contribution to this debate could look like:

**Pillar 1 — Technology-Neutral Risk Assessment:** The March 2026 U.S. joint guidance on tokenized securities established that capital treatment should reflect underlying asset risk, not the technology used to record it. Brazil could formally adopt this principle in its Consulta Pública 126 response and apply it to PSAV capital calibration, distinguishing between assets based on liquidity, counterparty risk, and AML/CFT infrastructure rather than asset category alone.

**Pillar 2 — Traceability as a Risk Mitigant:** Public blockchain assets offer a structural advantage that traditional assets do not: permanent, auditable transaction history. A PSAV holding spot Bitcoin on a public blockchain operates in a fundamentally more transparent environment than a bank holding opaque securitization tranches. Brazil's framework — which already mandates on-chain monitoring, Travel Rule compliance, and self-hosted wallet identification — has the operational infrastructure to make traceability a formal input into risk-weight calibration. This is a position that aligns with the Bitcoin Policy Institute's "Basel's 1250% Mistake" paper and with the senators' technology-neutral principle, while preserving Brazil's commitment to rigorous AML/CFT oversight.

**Pillar 3 — Dynamic Review Mechanism:** Rather than anchoring capital requirements to a specific Basel standard that is actively under revision, Consulta Pública 126 could incorporate a structured review mechanism — for example, a formal BACEN assessment triggered by any material change to BCBS 405 or to U.S. federal Basel implementation rules. This mechanism would allow Brazil's capital framework to evolve in alignment with international standards without requiring a full legislative or regulatory process each time the global baseline shifts.

## VI. The AML Dimension: Capital Adequacy and Compliance Infrastructure

I flag one dimension of this debate that has not received adequate attention in the policy literature: the direct relationship between capital adequacy and AML compliance capacity.

AML/CFT compliance infrastructure is not free. Effective transaction monitoring, KYT systems, sanctions screening, Travel Rule compliance tools, on-chain analytics, and human investigation capacity all require sustained operational investment. For PSAVs operating under Brazil's framework, these are not optional — they are mandatory conditions of licensing.

A capital requirement calibrated significantly above the level justified by actual risk does not merely constrain investment activity. It constrains the operational resources available to fund the compliance infrastructure that the same regulatory framework requires. A PSAV that must allocate disproportionate capital against digital asset holdings has fewer resources to invest in the KYT platforms, blockchain analytics subscriptions, and compliance personnel that Resolution 519 and IN 739 mandate.

I apply FATF Recommendation 1 to this observation: the Risk-Based Approach requires not only that obligations be proportionate to risk, but that regulated entities have the operational capacity to discharge those obligations effectively. Capital calibration that is systematically above risk-adjusted levels can undermine the very compliance infrastructure that makes the regulatory framework function.

This is not an argument for lower standards. It is an argument for accurately calibrated standards — which is precisely the position that Brazil's BACEN, as a sophisticated regulator with deep Basel engagement, is well positioned to articulate in the international policy debate.

---

## VII. Investigative Conclusion

I summarize my findings as follows:

The Basel 1,250% risk weight for digital assets is entering active global revision, driven by bipartisan U.S. Senate pressure, unified industry advocacy from more than 100 major digital asset firms, and the internal logic of the Digital Asset Market Clarity Act — which presupposes meaningful bank participation in digital asset markets that the current Basel framework makes economically impractical.

Brazil has constructed a rigorous, internationally aligned PSAV regulatory framework through Lei 14.478/2022, Resolutions 519/520/521, and IN 739. Consulta Pública 126, which proposes prudential capital rules aligned with Basel standards, is open at precisely the moment when those standards are being renegotiated.

I document this convergence as a strategic window rather than a regulatory risk. Brazil has the market scale, the regulatory sophistication, and the operational framework to contribute meaningfully to the international debate on how to calibrate capital requirements for digital assets that operate on transparent, auditable blockchain infrastructure under robust AML/CFT supervision.

The question I flag for further monitoring is whether Brazil's participation in Basel Committee discussions will reflect the operational reality of its own PSAV framework — which is built on traceability, continuous monitoring, and risk-proportionate oversight — as the international standard is revised.
